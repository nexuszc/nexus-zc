Tile Roofing Supplement Codes: A Comprehensive Guide for Roofing Contractors

Understanding Tile Roofing Specifics for Insurance Claims

Tile roofing presents unique challenges in the insurance claims process that differ significantly from asphalt shingle installations. Clay, concrete, and slate tiles carry substantially higher material costs, longer lead times, and require specialized installation expertise. Insurance adjusters frequently underestimate the true cost of tile roof replacement because they may lack familiarity with the material's inherent complexities. Understanding these nuances is critical for contractors seeking accurate supplements and fair claim settlements. Tile roofs also require underlayment specifications that exceed standard requirements, along with structural considerations that impact labor costs and timeline projections.

Essential Xactimate Codes for Tile Roofing Work

When inputting tile roofing line items into Xactimate, precision is paramount. The primary code for tile roof removal is typically found under "Roofing – Remove" categories. For clay tile removal, use codes that specify "clay tile – remove per square" to ensure accurate pricing. Concrete tile removal codes should reflect the material-specific weight and handling requirements. When replacing tiles, select codes that differentiate between new clay tile installation (typically 091-200 range) and concrete tile installation (091-300 range). Slate tile carries its own specialized codes due to premium pricing and installation difficulty.

Critical supplementary codes include roof decking replacement (091-500 series), which tile roofs frequently require due to structural damage underneath. Underlayment codes should reflect synthetic or high-quality felt specifications rather than standard roofing paper. Include flashing codes specific to tile installations, as these differ substantially from asphalt shingle specifications. Don't overlook ridge cap tiles, which often require custom fabrication and installation labor premiums. Document all line items with extended descriptions explaining why tile-specific codes and pricing apply rather than generic roofing codes.

Countering Common Adjuster Objections

Adjusters frequently challenge tile roofing estimates by suggesting alternative materials or citing depreciation on older tile roofs. Counter this by presenting the "Replacement Cost Value" principle found in most homeowner policies. If a tile roof is specified in the original construction, replacements must match the original material. Provide detailed photographic evidence showing the specific tile style, color matching requirements, and any custom work involved.

When adjusters claim tiles are "outdated" or suggest cheaper alternatives, reference the policy language prohibiting substitution of inferior materials. Document that comparable clay or concrete tiles meeting original specifications command premium pricing. If the adjuster provides a low estimate using generic roofing codes, prepare a detailed breakdown showing how tile-specific codes and labor multipliers justify your numbers. Request that adjusters use tile-specific databases rather than default asphalt shingle pricing.

Proper Documentation of Tile Roof Damage

Documentation serves as your evidence foundation. Photograph each damaged tile individually, showing both the damage and the tile's position on the roof. Capture wide-angle shots demonstrating the overall damage pattern. Document the tile manufacturer, style name, and color if visible. Take close-ups showing specific failure points—whether cracks, breaks, or lifting. Photograph the roof deck condition, noting any rot or structural issues requiring additional work. Create a damage map indicating the affected areas and quantifying the number of replacement tiles needed. Include comparison photos of undamaged areas to highlight deterioration severity.

Supplement Tracking Best Practices for Tile Roofing

Establish a tile-specific tracking system separating line items by category: material removal, structural repairs, underlayment, new tile installation, flashing, and finishing work. Maintain a running estimate spreadsheet with initial adjuster pricing versus your supplemented pricing, documenting the rationale for each increase. Save all Xactimate line item snapshots with notes explaining tile-specific code selections. Create a follow-up schedule for supplements, particularly after in-person roof inspections that often reveal additional damage. Keep copies of all correspondence with adjusters discussing tile roofing challenges.

By mastering tile roofing supplement codes and documentation standards, contractors can navigate insurance claims confidently while ensuring clients receive proper compensation for quality tile roof restoration.
