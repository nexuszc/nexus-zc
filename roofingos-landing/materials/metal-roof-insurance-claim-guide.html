Metal Roofing Insurance Claims: A Comprehensive Guide for Contractors

Metal roofing presents unique challenges in the insurance claim process. Understanding material specifications, proper documentation, and common adjuster objections is essential for maximizing claim recovery on metal roof projects. This guide provides contractors with the knowledge needed to navigate metal roofing insurance claims effectively.

Metal Roofing Specific Details Contractors Must Know

Metal roofing encompasses multiple system types, each with distinct characteristics affecting claim assessment. Standing seam, metal shingles, metal tiles, and corrugated panels each have different installation requirements, material costs, and failure patterns. Contractors must document the specific metal type, gauge, and coating during initial assessment. Establish whether the existing roof is structural or cosmetic metal roofing, as this affects replacement methodology and costs. Metal roofing longevity claims often exceed 50 years, which adjusters use to reduce replacement claims—document actual condition and failure mechanisms thoroughly. Additionally, note the fastening system type, as this influences both damage patterns and repair methodologies. Thermal expansion and contraction characteristics of metal roofing differ significantly from traditional materials, affecting how damage manifests and spreads across the system.

Xactimate Line Items and Codes for Metal Roofing

Accurate Xactimate coding ensures proper claim valuation. Use code 07310 for metal roof removal and disposal, specifying square footage and metal type. Code 07411 covers standing seam metal roof installation, while 07412 addresses metal shingle installation. For repairs, utilize 07415 for metal roof panel repairs and 07416 for fastener and seal replacement. Flashing repairs use code 07600, critical for metal roofing claims since flashing failures are common. When documenting materials, distinguish between aluminum, steel, copper, and zinc alloy specifications. Include labor codes 07310-L for removal and 07411-L for installation, ensuring regional labor rates are applied. Substrate preparation codes vary depending on whether existing decking requires repair or replacement—code 06200 for wood structural repair and 06400 for wood replacement. Always include applicable allowances for metal roof-specific accessories such as ridge caps (07330), valley treatments, and trim components that differ from standard asphalt roofing requirements.

Countering Common Adjuster Pushback

Adjusters frequently argue metal roofing is over-specified or unnecessary after damage to a standard roof. Counter this by establishing the original roof type from permits and photographs, demonstrating continuity of materials is required by most warranties and local building practices. When adjusters claim metal roofing is "upgrade" damage, clarify that replacement-in-kind is standard practice and that cosmetic upgrades differ from structural material replacement. Address longevity objections by providing evidence of actual damage mechanisms—rust perforation, fastener failure, or impact damage—rather than relying on age-based depreciation arguments. If adjusters undervalue metal materials, provide current supplier quotes and demonstrate code-compliance requirements. Challenge depreciation on metal roofing more aggressively; metal roofs typically depreciate slower than alternatives and often appreciate when properly maintained.

Documenting Metal Roofing Damage Properly

Metal roofing damage documentation requires specific photography angles. Capture fastener conditions, seam integrity, coating degradation, and rust patterns close-up. Document the overall system health beyond the immediate damage area. Include weather history and impact evidence. Thermal imaging can demonstrate seal failures and moisture intrusion invisible to standard photography.

Supplement Tracking for Metal Roofing Claims

Maintain detailed supplement logs tracking metal-specific discoveries: hidden structural damage, expanded flashing requirements, or substrate failures. Document each supplement with supporting photographs and material specifications, noting dates when information became available during installation phases.
