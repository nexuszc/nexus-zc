Tile roofing insurance claims present unique challenges that demand specialized knowledge from contractors. Unlike asphalt shingles, clay and concrete tile systems involve higher material costs, longer installation timelines, and more complex damage assessment protocols. Understanding these distinctions is essential for navigating the claims process successfully and protecting your bottom line.

Tile Roofing Specific Details Contractors Must Know

Tile roofing systems consist of interlocking clay or concrete tiles installed over battens and underlayment. The key distinction from other roofing types is that tile damage assessment requires evaluating both individual tiles and the underlying support structure. Insurance carriers often underestimate replacement costs because tile roofing is less common than asphalt systems, making education crucial during the claims process. Additionally, tile roofs typically last 50+ years, which insurers may reference to suggest partial replacements rather than full system replacement. Contractors must understand local building codes, as many jurisdictions now require complete roof system replacement regardless of tile lifespan when structural damage is present.

Xactimate Line Items and Codes for Tile Roofing

When preparing estimates in Xactimate, utilize the following critical codes: Roof Tile Removal (07-31-01), which covers tearoff labor; Roof Tile Installation (07-31-02), for new tile placement; Underlayment Installation (07-32-01), essential for tile systems; Batten Installation (07-21-01), specific to tile support structures; and Flashing Installation (07-46-01), for critical penetrations and edges. For clay tiles specifically, use code 07-31-01.10, and for concrete tiles, use 07-31-01.20. Include line items for substrate repair (07-11-01) when decking damage is evident. Always separate material and labor costs, as adjusters frequently negotiate these components independently. Document waste factors of 10-15% for tile work, which contractors often omit, creating significant profit erosion.

Common Insurance Adjuster Pushback and Counter-Strategies

Adjusters frequently challenge tile roofing claims with four primary objections. First, they claim matching existing tiles is unnecessary when only partial damage exists. Counter this by citing building code requirements for full roof replacement and explaining that partial replacements create visible color variations and potential structural incompatibility. Second, they argue that depreciation should apply heavily to older tile roofs. Respond with evidence that quality tile roofing maintains structural integrity regardless of age and that depreciation calculations should reflect actual condition, not mere age. Third, adjusters suggest using aftermarket or lower-grade tiles to reduce costs. Document why OEM matching is necessary for warranty purposes and structural compatibility. Fourth, they undervalue labor costs for tile installation. Provide detailed breakdowns showing the specialized skill required for tile placement and the time investment compared to standard roofing.

Documenting Tile Roofing Damage Properly

Comprehensive documentation is your strongest defense against claim denials. Photograph individual tiles from multiple angles, capturing breakage patterns and underlying damage. Document the underlayment condition separately, as this often determines whether partial or full replacement is necessary. Create a damage map indicating affected areas by percentage. Use close-up photography to show batten condition, fastener integrity, and any structural concerns. Include wide-angle shots demonstrating the relationship between damaged areas and surrounding structures. Obtain weather data from the claim date to establish causation. Test the deck integrity photographically, documenting soft spots or delamination. Retain physical samples of damaged tiles when possible for adjuster inspection.

Supplement Tracking Tips Specific to Tile Roofing

Tile roofing claims frequently require supplements due to hidden damage discovered during tearoff. Implement a systematic tracking system documenting initial estimates versus supplemental findings. Create a detailed pre-tearoff report identifying areas requiring closer inspection. During tearoff, photograph decking conditions, batten damage, and underlayment deterioration continuously. Submit supplements promptly with photo documentation of newly discovered damage. Maintain separate line items for original estimate versus supplemental work to streamline adjuster review. For tile roofing specifically, budget tearoff time to allow thorough inspection without rushing, as hidden damage significantly impacts final costs. Establish communication protocols with adjusters before tearoff begins, clarifying that additional damage discovery is standard practice in tile roofing claims.
