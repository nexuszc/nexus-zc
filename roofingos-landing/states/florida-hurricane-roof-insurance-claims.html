Florida Hurricane Claims for Roofing Contractors: A Practical Guide

Florida-Specific Requirements for Hurricane Claims

Florida's insurance landscape differs significantly from other states due to the constant threat of hurricanes. Before submitting any claim on behalf of a homeowner, roofing contractors must understand that Florida requires all insurance claims to be handled through the Florida Department of Financial Services (DFS). Additionally, contractors should know that they cannot legally pressure homeowners to file claims or handle claims processing directly without proper licensing. You'll need to work with licensed public adjusters or allow homeowners to manage their own claims through their insurance carriers.

Florida law also mandates that all roofing work following hurricane damage must meet current Florida Building Code standards. This often means upgrades beyond what the original roof required, and contractors must be prepared to discuss these additional costs with homeowners and their insurance adjusters upfront.

What You Need to Know Before Starting Jobs in Florida

First, ensure your roofing license is current and in good standing with the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR). Without a valid Florida roofing contractor license, you cannot legally bid on or complete roofing work in the state, even if you're licensed in other states.

Second, obtain proper insurance yourself. Florida roofing contractors typically need general liability, workers' compensation, and property damage insurance. After a major hurricane, demand for roofing services skyrockets, and you need proof of insurance before accessing most properties or working with insurance companies.

Third, understand that homeowners often have deductibles ranging from $1,000 to $5,000 or more. Many don't realize this until the claim is processed. Being transparent about deductible costs upfront prevents misunderstandings and lost jobs.

Common Pitfalls in Florida Roofing Claims

The biggest pitfall is underestimating scope-of-work. Inspectors and adjusters in Florida are thorough. If you estimate only partial replacement when full replacement is needed, you'll face callbacks and disputes. Always conduct comprehensive inspections and document everything photographically.

Another frequent mistake is failing to coordinate with insurance adjusters. Many contractors jump straight to work without waiting for the adjuster's assessment. This creates conflicts when the adjuster identifies damage the contractor missed or disputes the repair approach. Schedule adjuster inspections before starting work whenever possible.

Don't ignore minor existing damage that wasn't hurricane-related. Insurance companies won't cover pre-existing conditions. Document the condition of the roof before work begins, and clearly separate hurricane damage from older issues in your estimates.

Finally, avoid signing agreements that make you responsible for claim denials or deductibles. Your responsibility is quality workmanship within the scope approved by insurance.

Resources and Official Links

Visit the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) at myfloridalicense.com to verify licenses and access contractor requirements. The Florida Department of Financial Services (DFS) at floir.com provides information on insurance regulations and claim procedures. The Florida Building Code is available through the Florida Building Commission website, and contractors should reference the most current version regularly.
