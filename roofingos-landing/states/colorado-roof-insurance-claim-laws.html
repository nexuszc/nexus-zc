Colorado Insurance Claim Laws for Roofing Contractors

Understanding Colorado's Insurance Requirements

Colorado requires roofing contractors to carry specific insurance coverage before starting any residential work. The state mandates general liability insurance with minimum coverage limits, though these requirements can vary by municipality. Most Colorado cities require contractors to carry at least $1 million in general liability coverage. Additionally, if you have employees, workers' compensation insurance is mandatory in Colorado. Independent contractors may be exempt, but this determination depends on how they're classified under state law. Understanding these baseline requirements prevents costly penalties and work stoppages.

Colorado-Specific Claim Filing Requirements

Colorado has strict timelines for insurance claims related to roofing damage. Most homeowner insurance policies require damage claims to be filed within one to three years of the incident, but Colorado law recognizes that weather damage—particularly hail and wind—may not be immediately visible. When filing claims on behalf of customers, contractors must provide detailed documentation including photographs, written estimates, and inspection reports. The Colorado Division of Insurance enforces unfair claim settlement practices act, which means insurers cannot unreasonably delay claim decisions. Contractors should document all communications with insurance adjusters and keep detailed records of estimates provided to homeowners.

Critical Requirements Before Starting Colorado Jobs

Before accepting any roofing job in Colorado, verify your insurance certificates are current and properly listed with the homeowner's insurance company if required. You'll need a valid Colorado Roofing Contractor License from the State Licensing Board. Obtain pre-job inspections documented in writing, especially for hail or wind damage claims. Create a scope of work that aligns with insurance adjuster assessments—discrepancies between your estimate and the insurance company's evaluation can delay payments. Always provide homeowners with a written contract detailing what work will be completed, what the insurance will cover, and what the homeowner is responsible for financially.

Common Pitfalls Colorado Contractors Face

One major mistake is working without proper licensing verification. Colorado's licensing board actively investigates complaints, and unlicensed work can result in fines and legal action. Another pitfall is over-estimating repairs covered by insurance claims. If your quote exceeds what the insurance adjuster approved, you may end up covering the difference or facing disputes with homeowners. Colorado contractors often underestimate the complexity of hail damage claims, which require itemized documentation of each damaged area. Don't skip the step of obtaining homeowner consent before contacting their insurance company—this protects you legally and maintains client trust. Additionally, failing to pull proper permits for major roof replacements can result in fines and complications with future insurance claims on that property.

Essential Resources and Official Links

The Colorado Division of Insurance website (division.colorado.gov/insurance) provides regulatory guidance and complaint procedures. The Colorado Department of Regulatory Agencies oversees contractor licensing. The National Roofing Contractors Association maintains Colorado-specific resources and best practices. The Better Business Bureau Colorado chapter helps verify contractor credentials and customer complaint history.
