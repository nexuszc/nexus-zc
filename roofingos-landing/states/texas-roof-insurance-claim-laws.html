Texas Insurance Claim Laws for Roofing Contractors

Texas roofing contractors operate in a complex regulatory environment where insurance claims, licensing requirements, and consumer protection laws directly impact your business. Understanding these requirements isn't optional—it's essential to avoid costly penalties, lawsuits, and license suspension. This guide breaks down what you need to know to operate legally and profitably in Texas.

Texas-Specific Insurance Claim Requirements

Texas requires roofing contractors to maintain active general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage before beginning any work. The Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR) doesn't mandate a specific coverage amount, but your insurance policy must be current and verifiable. When filing insurance claims, you must provide accurate documentation including photos, damage assessments, and repair estimates within timeframes specified by the insurance carrier—typically 30 days for claim notification.

Texas Property Code Section 2703 requires contractors to include insurance information on all contracts before work begins. If you're handling insurance claim payments directly, you cannot legally cash checks made payable to both the homeowner and contractor without proper authorization. This is a common violation that triggers investigations from the Texas Attorney General's office.

Additionally, Texas has specific regulations about assignment of benefits (AOB) agreements. While AOBs aren't prohibited, they must be clearly disclosed in writing and signed by both parties before any work begins. Mishandling AOBs is a primary reason contractors face complaints with the TDLR.

What You Must Know Before Starting Jobs in Texas

First, verify your licensing status with TDLR before accepting any job. Texas requires roofing contractors to hold a current roofing license or operate under a licensed roofer's supervision. Check your license renewal date immediately—expired licenses result in automatic work stoppages and fines.

Second, always provide written contracts that include: detailed scope of work, labor and material costs, payment schedule, insurance information, and timeline for completion. Verbal agreements or vague estimates create disputes that damage insurance claims later. Texas courts enforce detailed contracts consistently.

Third, photograph everything before, during, and after work. Insurance companies require documented evidence of existing damage and completed repairs. Poor documentation is the top reason claims get denied or delayed in Texas.

Fourth, understand that homeowners have 30 days to file a claim with their insurance carrier. Don't promise customers quick claim approvals—insurance carriers control timelines, not contractors.

Common Pitfalls in Texas

The most dangerous mistake is depositing insurance checks without proper authorization. Texas law prohibits contractors from being sole signatories on insurance claim checks. Always require homeowner signatures for claim deposits.

Another frequent issue is working without an updated certificate of insurance on file. Many contractors believe verbal confirmation is sufficient—it's not. Provide updated certificates to property managers and general contractors in writing.

Failing to obtain permits for roofing work is extremely common in Texas. Many jurisdictions require permits for roof replacement. Operating without them violates local code and voids insurance coverage in claim disputes.

Finally, contractors often misunderstand deductible responsibility. You cannot legally waive or absorb homeowner deductibles as a marketing tactic in Texas. This constitutes insurance fraud.

Resources and Official Links
